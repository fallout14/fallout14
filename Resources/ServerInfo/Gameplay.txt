[color=#a4885c][b]Understanding IC and OOC[/b][/color]

If you are new to roleplay or unfamiliar with IC and OOC concepts, please read this section carefully.

SS14 is a roleplaying game at its foundation. Each round, you take on the role of a character you created in the lobby. You select their name, background, and job preferences before joining the round.

When you are actively playing your character in a round, you are [b]IC (In Character)[/b].
This means:

• You act as your character would act within the game world.
• You do not remember previous rounds.
• You do not know anything from outside the current round unless it is revealed in character.
• You behave as if you are truly a member of the station crew.

[b]OOC (Out of Character)[/b] refers to anything outside the game world.
This includes:

• OOC chat
• Discord discussions
• Conversations with friends
• Streams or external communication

Information learned OOC must never influence your IC actions.

Examples of prohibited behavior:
• Someone on Discord reveals who the traitor is and you act on it in game.
• A friend tells you their location while dying and you rush to save them.
• You read OOC chat about an event and prepare for it IC before it happens.

This is called [b]metagaming[/b] and is a serious violation that may result in a ban.

If you believe someone is using outside information in character, report it through admin help (ahelp) or the appropriate report channels.

[b]Summary:[/b]
While a round is active, keep round-related discussion inside the round. Do not share or act on external information until the round has ended.

[color=#a4885c][b]Antagonists, RDM, and Self-Antagging[/b][/color]

[b]Antagonist Selection[/b]

If you enable antagonist roles in your character preferences, you are only opting into the chance to receive one. Selection is random and not guaranteed.

If chosen, you will:
• Receive a notification in chat.
• Be given objectives accessible through your character interface.

Being selected as an antagonist does not remove roleplay expectations.

[b]Playing an Antagonist[/b]

Your goal is to complete your assigned objectives. These objectives may involve sabotage, theft, escape, or sometimes death.

However:

• You are not automatically permitted to kill indiscriminately.
• Actions should still fit the setting and roleplay expectations.
• Chaos for its own sake is discouraged.

Violence can be valid, but it should serve a purpose and contribute to the round’s story rather than simply ending it.

[b]Responding to Antagonists[/b]

If you suspect someone is an antagonist:

• Inform Security through proper IC channels.
• Protect yourself if necessary.
• Remember that arrest and due process are preferable when possible.

Survival is important, but escalation should still make sense in context.

[b]RDM (Random Deathmatch)[/b]

RDM is attacking or killing someone without roleplay justification or proper escalation.

Examples include:
• Killing someone without interaction or motive.
• Attacking someone purely for amusement.

If you are attacked and unsure whether it was valid, you may contact staff through ahelp when appropriate.

[b]Self-Antagging[/b]

Self-antagging occurs when a player who is not an assigned antagonist deliberately behaves like one.

Examples include:
• Attempted murder without antagonist status.
• Sabotaging critical systems without reason.
• Breaking into high-security areas without roleplay justification.

Minor lawbreaking does not automatically qualify as self-antagging. However, major criminal actions without antagonist status will be treated seriously.

If you are not selected as an antagonist, you are expected to perform your job and participate in the round as a normal crew member.
